Recommended reading for an aspiring medical student

When Breath Becomes Air
by Paul Kalanithi

This one hits differently than any other medicine book because it's written by a neurosurgeon who gets diagnosed with terminal cancer in his 30s, right at the peak of his training. So you're reading someone who spent his whole life preparing to help people face death, and then suddenly has to face it himself.

This book asks the deeper question underneath the job, "Why does it matter to be there with people at the hardest moments of their lives?" This is a book written by a brilliant man who faced death (without the anchor of faith), and it shows both how far human wisdom can take you and where it reaches its limits. For a Christian reader, that's actually meaningful to witness someone straining toward transcendant meaning in dire circumstances.

Recommended reading for living a meaningful life

Boundaries: When to Say Yes, How to Say No
by Henry Cloud

Want a manual for a life you actually want? This book is about one of the most useful skills nobody ever teaches you how to say no without guilt, how to stop taking responsibility for other people's emotions, and how to protect your own time and energy without being selfish about it.

It's written from a Christian framework, so the foundation is solid. But the practical stuff applies to every relationship in your life: your parents, your teammates, your friends, eventually your patients.

Here's why it matters right now specifically: you're at a school that will take everything you give it and ask for more. The people who burn out are the ones who never learned where they end and where everything else begins. The people who last (and actually enjoy the journey) figured out how to draw those lines.

7 Habits of Highly Effective People
by Stephen Covey

This is probably the most recommended self-help book of all time, which makes it easy to dismiss. Don't.

The reason it's lasted is because Covey isn't giving you productivity hacks or morning routines, he's making an argument that the way you see yourself and the world determines everything else. Before you can manage your time, you have to know what you actually value. Before you can lead other people, you have to be able to lead yourself.

For someone who wants to be a doctor, who competes in water polo, and who's building something with his content — you're already juggling more than most people your age. This book gives you a framework for making sure all of that is actually pointing somewhere you chose, not just somewhere you ended up.

Tip: Read Habit 2 ("Begin with the End in Mind") first. If that one lands, the rest will too.

Becoming a high-performing student

How To Become A Straight A Student
by Cal Newport

This book has a misleading title. It sounds like someone's going to tell you to study more and sleep less. It's actually the opposite.

Cal Newport interviewed hundreds of top students at elite universities and found that the ones getting the best grades were almost never the ones studying the longest. They had figured out how to work in focused, intense bursts and then actually stop.. leaving time for everything else in their lives. The students killing themselves with hours of unfocused studying were getting worse results.

For someone at Iolani, that's worth thinking about with for a second. The game isn't about more hours. It's about figuring out which hours actually move the needle and making them count.

The practical stuff in here is also genuinely useful: how to take notes so you actually retain things, how to handle assignments without letting them pile up in your head, how to study for tests without cramming the night before. It's not flashy but it works, and it's the kind of thing that frees up time for spearfishing and water polo and everything else that actually makes your life worth living. Read this one with a notebook next to you.

Organization & Time Management

How I Organize My Busy Schedule (Student Edition)
Koi
Most productivity advice is for adults with assistants. This is for students. The creators are two medical doctors who lived through the grind. Their system: know how long tasks take, and capture everything outside your head.
How To Learn Anything FASTER
Ali Abdaal
This explains the "forgetting curve." It’s not a memory problem; it’s a method problem. The fix is Spaced Repetition and Active Recall. The research behind why it works is fascinating for a future doctor.
How to Study for Exams
Ali Abdaal
A full course (originally paid) given for free. It breaks down how to actually understand material instead of just recognizing it. Watch the section on the Feynman Method—explaining things simply to test understanding.
The Bible’s Advice for Beating Procrastination
Redeeming Productivity
This reframes the question from 'how do I force myself' to 'what does it mean to steward the gifts God gave me.' Understanding the root of avoidance is more useful than just trying harder.
